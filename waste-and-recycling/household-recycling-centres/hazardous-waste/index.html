Types of hazardous household waste

Waste is hazardous if it contains anything that is harmful to humans or the environment. Hazardous waste cannot be disposed of in your household bins and must be taken to a recycling centre that is equipped to handle it safely.

The main types of hazardous household waste accepted at Buckinghamshire recycling centres are:

  • Asbestos
  • Garden chemicals and fuel
  • Plasterboard
  • Non-native invasive plants

Recycling centres that accept hazardous waste

Not all centres are equipped to handle hazardous materials. The following six sites accept asbestos, plasterboard and garden chemicals:

  • Amersham recycling centre
  • Aston Clinton recycling centre
  • Aylesbury recycling centre
  • Beaconsfield recycling centre
  • High Wycombe recycling centre
  • Langley recycling centre

Asbestos

Asbestos is a naturally occurring fibrous material that was widely used in building construction for insulation and fireproofing. Its use is now banned in the UK, but buildings constructed before the year 2000 may still contain asbestos.

When asbestos is damaged or disturbed, it can release microscopic fibres into the air. Breathing these fibres in can cause serious and potentially fatal lung conditions. Only bonded (non-friable) asbestos is accepted at recycling centres — loose or crumbling asbestos must be removed by a licensed specialist.

Asbestos waste permit

You must apply for an asbestos waste permit before visiting a recycling centre. A maximum of three bin-liner-sized bags, or up to four sheets measuring 2 x 1 metre, will be accepted per visit.

Apply for an asbestos waste permit

Conditions of disposal

Only bonded asbestos is accepted. Before your visit:

  • Remove all wood, glass, plastic and metal from the asbestos
  • Double-bag the material in heavy-duty polythene bags
  • Seal the bags securely and label them as asbestos

What to expect when you arrive

A site attendant will direct you to the designated asbestos container. The material will be dampened down to suppress any fibres before disposal. Please allow extra time for your visit as this process takes longer than standard waste disposal.

Private asbestos removal

If you are unsure whether a material contains asbestos, or if the asbestos is in poor condition, do not disturb it. Contact a specialist licensed contractor to carry out a survey and arrange safe removal. The Asbestos Removal Contractors Association (ARCA) can help you find an approved contractor.

Garden chemicals and fuel

Some products are perfectly safe to use in everyday life but are considered hazardous when it comes to disposing of them. Garden chemicals and fuels must never be disposed of in your household bins, down the drain, in sinks or in toilets — this is illegal and can cause serious harm to the environment.

Types of garden chemicals accepted

The following garden chemicals are accepted at the six hazardous waste centres, in their original containers, up to a maximum of 5 litres per visit:

  • Weed killers
  • Insecticides
  • Fungicide sprays

Disposal of fuel

Fuel including paraffin, petrol and diesel is accepted free of charge. Fuel must be brought in its original container. Do not mix different fuel types.

Plasterboard

Plasterboard (also known as drywall or gypsum board) is used in the construction of internal walls and ceilings. It is classified as hazardous waste and must be kept separate from other waste streams at recycling centres, as mixing it with other waste can release toxic hydrogen sulphide gas in landfill.

A limited amount of plasterboard is accepted free of charge if you hold a valid DIY waste permit. Charges may apply for larger quantities.

Non-native plants

Plants that have been introduced to a place where they do not naturally occur are known as non-native species. Some invasive non-native plants are classified as controlled waste under UK law and cannot be disposed of in your household bins or garden waste collections.

The following plants must be brought to a recycling centre in sealed bags and disposed of as controlled waste:

  • Japanese knotweed
  • Giant hogweed
  • Himalayan balsam
  • Rhododendron ponticum

For guidance on identifying and managing invasive plants, visit GOV.UK guidance on invasive non-native plants.
